---
title: "Decisions and Data"
date: 2024-03-08
---

The hardest part of data work isn't the analysis. It's the gap between having an answer and changing what someone does.

Most data projects fail at this gap. The analysis is technically correct. The finding is real. And then nothing happens. The organization absorbs the information, files it somewhere, and continues doing what it was already doing.

This isn't cynicism about data. It's a structural problem. Analysis lands in one place—a report, a dashboard, a presentation—and decisions happen somewhere else, on different timelines, with different pressures. Bridging that gap requires something most data teams aren't hired to do: understanding how decisions actually get made.

---

There's a useful distinction between decisions and conclusions. A conclusion is something you can know. A decision requires someone to act, which means it touches accountability, resources, and organizational politics that no dataset can resolve.

Data scientists often produce conclusions and call the job done. The useful work—helping people move from "we know this" to "we'll do this differently"—is harder to scope and harder to measure. It looks less like analysis and more like facilitation.

The organizations that get the most from data aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones that have figured out how to connect findings to the people who can do something about them.
