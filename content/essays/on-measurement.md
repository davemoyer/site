---
title: "On Measurement"
date: 2024-01-15
---

There's a version of measurement that serves knowledge, and a version that serves compliance. They look similar from the outside but lead to very different places.

Education is full of the second kind. We measure because we're required to, or because it signals seriousness, or because other organizations do it. The result is a mountain of data that nobody uses to make decisions—and a corresponding erosion of trust in measurement itself.

The question worth asking isn't "what can we measure?" but "what decisions do we need to make, and what would help us make them better?" That reframe sounds minor. In practice it changes almost everything.

When you start from decisions, measurement becomes concrete. You're no longer counting for its own sake—you're trying to reduce uncertainty about something specific. The data either helps or it doesn't. Feedback is fast.

---

I've seen organizations spend years building reporting infrastructure that nobody opens. The effort isn't wasted out of laziness; it's wasted because the infrastructure was designed to exist, not to be used.

Good measurement is almost always narrower than people expect. A few well-defined questions, collected consistently, connected to people who have the authority to act on them. Everything else is noise.
